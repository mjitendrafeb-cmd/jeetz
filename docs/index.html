2026-06-14 mixed Financial Stability
International_Monetary_Fund_Systemic_Banking_Crises_Database_1970.pdf
This IMF Working Paper updates the Laeven-Valencia systemic banking crises database through 2025, recording 164 crises since 1970 (including 13 new and 3 borderline episodes), using a hybrid textual-analysis and expert-evaluation methodology. The key finding is that despite COVID-19 and the post-pandemic inflation surge, no widespread systemic banking crises materialized—reflecting post-GFC capital/liquidity reforms and prompt policy support—while the 2023 US (SVB, Signature, First Republic) and Swiss (Credit Suisse) failures fell short of the systemic threshold yet underscored persistent interest-rate and deposit-flight vulnerabilities.

📋 Key Takeaways
  • The database now covers 164 systemic banking crises over 1970-2025, with 13 new episodes added since the 2020 update and 3 borderline cases (Nicaragua 2018, Vietnam 2022, Sri Lanka 2023).
  • A banking crisis is defined by two conditions: significant signs of financial distress (bank runs, losses, liquidations) AND significant policy intervention (at least 3 of 6 specified measures).
  • Severe losses alone can date a crisis: NPLs above 20% of total loans, bank closures of at least 20% of banking-system assets, or fiscal restructuring costs exceeding 5% of GDP.
  • The methodology now integrates textual analysis of IMF Article IV reports, TA reports, and FSSAs (2017-2025) using a dictionary of ~250 banking-stress terms, combined with manual expert verification.
  • Most new episodes originated from shocks OUTSIDE the financial sector—notably the post-2014 oil price collapse hitting oil producers like Angola, Chad, Azerbaijan, Equatorial Guinea, and Republic of Congo.
  • The 2023 US regional bank failures (SVB, Signature, First Republic) and Credit Suisse's takeover did NOT meet the systemic crisis definition because early intervention prevented contagion and thresholds were not crossed.
  • SVB and Signature both had about 90% uninsured deposits; together with First Republic they held US$367 billion in deposits (2.0% of total US bank deposits).
  • Credit Suisse represented 14.2% of Swiss banking sector assets; SNB liquidity support reached 4.5% of total banking system liabilities, just below the 5% systemic threshold.
  • US peak liquidity support during 2023 was about 1% of total US bank liabilities—well below the 5% threshold—with no fiscal restructuring costs.
  • Post-GFC reforms (Basel III releasable buffers—CCyB, capital conservation buffer—and the LCR) materially diversified the crisis-response toolkit and contributed to avoiding a COVID-era crisis wave.
  • Liquidity support is pervasive across all crises; the median peak liquidity support ratio reaches 20% of deposits and foreign liabilities.
  • High-income countries experience longer crises (often 5+ years) with greater output losses but lower fiscal costs, while low/middle-income crises are shorter (typically ≤4 years) with higher fiscal costs and much higher NPLs.
  • Median gross fiscal cost is 6.7% of GDP (high income) versus 7.5% of GDP (low/middle income).
  • Median peak NPLs are ~11.1% (high income) versus 30% (low/middle income).
  • Median cumulative output loss is 31.75% of GDP (high income) versus 10.8% (low/middle income), reflecting deeper financial systems in advanced economies.
  • Only Argentina (4), Chad (3), DR Congo (3), Nicaragua (3), and Ukraine (3) experienced more than two crises in the period.
  • Significant blanket guarantees on bank liabilities are more common in high-income countries, possibly due to stronger institutions, more fiscal space, or lower FX-denominated liabilities.
  • Crisis end dates are defined as the year before both real GDP and real credit growth turn positive for two consecutive years; durations are truncated at five years.
  • Lebanon's 2019 crisis had estimated overall financial-system losses of about US$70 billion (more than 300% of estimated 2022 GDP).
⚠️ Risk Analysis
  • Interest-rate risk: The 2023 episodes highlighted that rapid rate increases impaired bank securities portfolios (SVB), creating mark-to-market solvency pressures even in sophisticated systems.
  • Liquidity/deposit-flight risk: Bank runs occurred at unprecedented speed due to high uninsured-deposit concentration (~90% at SVB/Signature) and ease of online withdrawals—SVB lost US$42 billion (~25% of deposits) in one day; this signals heightened modern run dynamics.
  • Credit risk/asset quality: Peak NPLs reach a median of 30% in low/middle-income crises and 11% in high-income, indicating severe portfolio deterioration; commodity-price shocks (oil) are a key channel impairing bank balance sheets.
  • Sovereign-fiscal risk: Bank rescues impose substantial fiscal costs (median ~7% of GDP, ranging up to ~57% historically for Argentina/Indonesia/Thailand), creating bank-sovereign feedback loops; Lebanon's losses exceeding 300% of GDP illustrate extreme tail outcomes.
  • Contagion/spillover risk: Crises are rarely single-country events—common global/regional shocks and cross-border exposures propagate stress (Latin America 1980s, Asian crisis, GFC).
  • Concentration risk: Wholesale-funding and uninsured-deposit concentration amplified run speed and vulnerability in the 2023 failures.
  • Regulatory/forbearance risk: NPL comparability is limited by regulatory forbearance and non-homogeneous definitions across countries, potentially understating distress.
  • Solvency-versus-liquidity misdiagnosis risk: Low/middle-income countries' prolonged reliance on liquidity support reflects difficulty distinguishing temporary liquidity shocks from solvency problems and lack of fiscal backstops, which can delay resolution.
  • FX/blanket-guarantee credibility risk: Guarantees on foreign-currency-denominated bank liabilities depend on available FX liquidity, undermining the credibility of blanket guarantees in low/middle-income countries.
  • Operational/model risk: Crisis dating depends on textual-analysis tools and judgmental thresholds, introducing potential for missed or misclassified episodes.
💡 Analyst Implications
  • Monitor banks with high uninsured/wholesale deposit concentrations and large unrealized losses on held securities as elevated run-risk candidates—a key idiosyncratic watch-list trigger post-2023.
  • Sector view: global banking resilience improved post-GFC due to Basel III buffers and LCR, supporting a relatively constructive baseline, but tail vulnerabilities to rate and confidence shocks persist.
  • Watch commodity-exporting/oil-dependent sovereigns and their banking systems as crisis-prone given the demonstrated transmission of oil-price shocks to bank balance sheets.
  • Use NPL >20%, bank closures >20% of assets, and restructuring costs >5% of GDP as concrete escalation thresholds for flagging systemic deterioration.
  • For high-income bank exposures, anticipate longer crisis duration and larger output drag (median ~32% of GDP) even where direct fiscal costs are contained.
  • For low/middle-income sovereign and bank exposures, anticipate higher fiscal-cost and NPL burdens, stressing the bank-sovereign nexus relevant to sovereign ratings.
  • Treat extensive liquidity support (>5% of deposits/foreign liabilities, more than doubling) and deposit freezes/bank holidays as confirmation signals that distress has become systemic.
  • Lebanon's >300%-of-GDP losses warrant continued distressed/default treatment of its banking and sovereign exposures.
  • Sri Lanka (2023), Vietnam (2022), and Nicaragua (2018) flagged as borderline—keep on heightened surveillance for potential escalation to full systemic status.

Motilal Oswal reiterates a BUY on Nuvama Wealth with a SoTP-based target price of INR1,860 (23% upside), citing one of the cheapest valuations in the capital market ecosystem at 19x FY28E P/E. The diversified wealth and capital markets franchise is expected to deliver 18%/19% revenue/PAT CAGR over FY26-28, driven by robust MPIS-led wealth flows, record net new money, and a recovering Asset Services segment, with recent regulatory changes deemed manageable.

📋 Key Takeaways
  • Nuvama is a diversified play across UHNI Wealth (Nuvama Private ~24% of FY26 revenue), mid-market Wealth (~31%), Capital Markets & Asset Services (~43%), and a nascent loss-making AMC/Alternates platform (~2%).
  • Overall revenue/PAT is expected to grow at 18%/19% CAGR over FY26-28; consolidated revenue rising from INR31.2b (FY26) to INR41.9b (FY28E) and PAT from INR10.5b to INR14.3b.
  • Wealth segment (mid-market) is the most resilient earnings anchor; managed average client assets of ~INR1.1t (up 14% YoY) and posted record net new money of INR147b in FY26.
  • Managed Products and Investment Solutions (MPIS) contributed INR89.2b (~61%) of NNM in FY26 and ~59% of total revenue, supporting higher trail income, stickiness, and lower cyclicality.
  • Wealth segment expected to deliver ~17% CAGR in average client assets and ~16% revenue CAGR over FY26-28, with ~200bp margin expansion.
  • Loan book expanded to INR49b from INR28b at start of FY26; management targets 20-30% loan book growth in FY27, supporting NII.
  • Wealth retention stable at ~90bp; cost-to-income ratio in mid-60% range and improving gradually (FY26 65.7% to FY28E 64.0%).
  • Nuvama Private (UHNI/family-office) serves 4,750+ families through 145+ RMs; FY26 revenue grew 24% YoY with 32% YoY surge in recurring revenue; ARR assets up 21% YoY to INR540b; retention at 89bp.
  • Nuvama Private expected to deliver 22%/20% CAGR in ARR AUM/total revenue over FY26-28 with ~150bp margin expansion; FY26 flows of INR141b excl. heldaway assets.
  • Offshore expansion progressing: Dubai office (7-8 RMs) achieved breakeven, new Singapore office added; second Dubai location outside DIFC planned; offshore is ~5% of private revenue and expected to grow.
  • Capital Markets & Asset Services (~43% of FY26 revenue) poised for steady rebound; Asset Services hit a new revenue peak in 4QFY26 after a major client exit during the year.
  • Asset Services retention improved to 2.5bp but average clearing assets fell 28% YoY to ~INR291b after client exit; franchise services 275+ institutional/corporate clients with high stickiness.
  • Capital Markets franchise revenue declined 19% YoY in FY26 due to F&O regulations; ECM/IB in slowdown phase from IPO slowdown, while fixed income remains stable; CIR held at ~38%.
  • Segment expected to deliver ~15% revenue CAGR over FY26-28 with operating margins stable at ~63%.
  • SEBI's final MF TER regulations implemented brokerage caps of 6bp (cash) and 2bp (derivatives), milder than proposed; no significant earnings impact expected.
  • AMC & Alternates platform is strategically important but loss-making; fee-paying AUM grew 42% YoY to ~INR112b in FY26 but revenue declined 4% YoY; operating losses widened to ~INR326m.
  • AMC management estimates NNM potential of INR70-80b next year with management fees of ~1.5-1.75%; AMC license enables expansion into SIF and retail-lite offerings; revenue expected at 56% CAGR over FY26-28.
  • PAT estimates cut by 4% each for FY27/FY28 due to MTM impact on wealth AUM from volatile equity markets and IE-IB slowdown.
  • RoE strong and stable at ~27.5% (FY26) to ~27% (FY28E); PAT margin ~33.6-34.1%.
  • SoTP valuation yields INR1,860 TP, with Asset Services valued highest at INR118.2b (20x P/E), Nuvama Wealth at INR91.2b (26x P/E), and Nuvama Private at INR83.6b (30x P/E).
  • India structural tailwinds: fastest millionaire addition globally; expected to contribute USD2t to global financial wealth among emerging markets (BCG 2026 report).
⚠️ Risk Analysis
  • Regulatory risk: RBI tightening on securities lending could increase cost of funds for the wealth loan book; impact deemed not significant but a monitoring item given 20-30% targeted loan growth.
  • Regulatory risk: RBI restriction on borrowings for proprietary trading; limited direct impact on Asset Services as clients are mainly FPIs who don't borrow from Indian banks, but a possible second-order volume impact.
  • Regulatory risk: STT hike on F&O (Union Budget 2026) may cause second-order volume impact on asset services volumes; FPIs can absorb relatively better given lower cost of funds.
  • Regulatory risk: SEBI MF TER brokerage caps (6bp cash, 2bp derivatives) and F&O regulations already pressuring Capital Markets revenue (-19% YoY in FY26), though milder than proposed.
  • Market risk: MTM impact on wealth management AUM from volatile equity markets directly drove a 4% PAT estimate cut for FY27/FY28; earnings sensitive to equity market cycles.
  • Concentration/operational risk: Asset Services suffered a 28% YoY decline in average clearing assets to ~INR291b following exit of a single large client, demonstrating client concentration vulnerability in the clearing/custody business.
  • Cyclicality/market risk: Capital Markets segment retains high cyclical sensitivity—ECM/IB hit by IPO slowdown and reduced institutional brokerage rates; transactional revenue in Private segment is inherently lumpy (74% of private average client assets are transactional).
  • Operational/execution risk: AMC/Alternates platform remains loss-making (operating losses widened to ~INR326m) and dependent on private-market fee timing, episodic exits, and public-market redemptions, creating near-term earnings volatility.
  • Funding/liquidity risk: Borrowings rose to INR115.4b in FY26 from INR78.4b in FY25 to fund loan book growth; rising cost of funds from regulatory tightening could pressure NII spreads.
  • Execution risk: RM workforce reshaping (replacing existing RMs with bank-trained professionals) and GenAI advisory pilots carry execution risk if productivity gains do not materialize.
💡 Analyst Implications
  • BUY rating reiterated with INR1,860 TP (23% upside) implying 24x FY28E P/E; positive sector view on Indian wealth management franchises.
  • Monitor loan book growth (targeted 20-30% in FY27) and the impact of RBI securities lending rules on cost of funds — a covenant/spread watch item given borrowings rose to INR115.4b.
  • Watch Asset Services clearing asset recovery and retention normalization (2.5bp) as the key indicator of post-client-exit stabilization; client concentration risk warrants monitoring.
  • Track AMC/Alternates path to breakeven — operating losses widening (-INR326m) is a near-term drag; AMC license rollout and fund deployment are catalysts to monitor.
  • Monitor IE-IB/ECM revenue trajectory amid IPO slowdown and brokerage rate compression as a downside trigger for the Capital Markets segment.
  • Regulatory developments (STT hike, F&O caps, proprietary trading borrowing restrictions) are watch items for second-order volume impact even if first-order earnings impact is muted.
  • High and stable RoE (~27%) with improving ARR/recurring revenue mix supports credit quality and earnings durability — favorable for a financials-sector exposure view.
