Executive Summary
  • IMF Working Paper (Laeven & Valencia, 2026) updates the systemic banking crises database to cover 1970-2025, recording 164 crises (including borderline cases), with 13 new episodes since the 2020 update.
  • Most new episodes stem from non-financial shocks—particularly the late-2014 oil price collapse hitting oil exporters (Angola, Chad, Azerbaijan, Equatorial Guinea)—rather than financial-sector origins.
  • The 2023 turmoil (SVB, Signature, First Republic in the US; Credit Suisse in Switzerland) did NOT meet the systemic crisis threshold, as prompt intervention contained contagion and liquidity support stayed below the 5% threshold.
  • Despite COVID-19, supply shocks and the inflation surge, no wave of systemic crises materialized—credited to post-GFC Basel III buffers (CCyB, capital conservation buffer), LCR, and decisive policy support.
  • High-income crises tend to last longer with larger output losses (median ~31.75% of GDP) but lower median fiscal costs and NPLs; low/middle-income crises show higher NPLs (median 30%) and shorter durations.
Key Takeaways & Analyst Lens
Key Takeaway Analyst Lens
Post-GFC regulatory reforms (Basel III releasable buffers, LCR) materially strengthened banking-system resilience and diversified the crisis-response toolkit, preventing a pandemic-era wave of systemic crises. Validates that well-capitalized, liquidity-buffered banks can absorb severe macro shocks without state rescue—supportive for bank sector credit profiles in jurisdictions with full Basel III implementation. Opportunity to differentiate issuers by buffer headroom and CCyB releasability. Monitor whether buffers are actually released in downturns versus hoarded, and whether weaker-implementing jurisdictions remain vulnerable; rating implications skew stable-to-positive for compliant banks, negative for laggards.
The 2023 failures (SVB, Signature, First Republic, Credit Suisse) demonstrated that fast deposit runs—driven by uninsured/wholesale deposit concentration and digital withdrawals—can fell individual banks even in advanced systems, yet swift official action prevented systemic escalation. Interest-rate risk in held-to-maturity portfolios plus concentrated uninsured funding is now a front-line credit risk, especially for mid-sized banks lacking G-SIB scrutiny. Risk: speed of modern runs compresses reaction time for both supervisors and analysts. Rating implication—penalize deposit concentration and unhedged duration risk; reward diversified, insured, sticky funding. Monitor uninsured deposit ratios, AFS/HTM unrealized losses, and deposit beta.
New crisis episodes are increasingly driven by external real-economy shocks (notably the oil price collapse) feeding into bank balance sheets, rather than endogenous financial excess. For commodity-dependent and concentrated economies, sovereign and banking risk are tightly linked through the commodity channel—bank asset quality is hostage to terms-of-trade swings. Opportunity to use commodity price scenarios as leading indicators of banking stress. Monitor oil/commodity exposure concentration in loan books, FX-denominated liabilities, and sovereign-bank doom-loop dynamics in frontier markets.
Crisis outcomes diverge sharply by income level: high-income crises last longer with larger output losses but lower NPLs and fiscal costs; low/middle-income crises show much higher NPLs (median 30% vs 11%) but shorter durations. Implies different recovery and loss-given-default assumptions by jurisdiction class. Larger, deeper financial systems transmit disruption more forcefully to GDP, while EM crises hit asset quality harder but resolve faster. Rating implication—calibrate sovereign and bank recovery timelines and through-the-cycle loss assumptions to income tier. Monitor fiscal space for backstops, as lack of a fiscal backstop prolongs reliance on liquidity over recapitalization.
Extensive central-bank liquidity support is the near-universal first response to crises (median peak liquidity support ~20% of deposits/foreign liabilities), but prolonged reliance can mask solvency problems. Heavy liquidity provision is a signal of stress, not stabilization—analysts should not read central-bank lifelines as resolution. Risk: low/middle-income countries lean on liquidity far longer before recapitalizing, delaying loss recognition and inflating eventual fiscal costs. Monitor the transition from liquidity support to recapitalization/restructuring as a key inflection in crisis trajectory and fiscal-cost build-up.

Entity Impact
Silicon Valley Bank, Signature Bank, First Republic (US regional banks) Failed in 2023 due to concentrated uninsured/wholesale deposits and rapid digital runs; resolved via FDIC (systemic risk exception, full uninsured-depositor protection; First Republic sold to JP Morgan Chase). Did not meet systemic crisis criteria.
Credit Suisse / UBS (Switzerland) G-SIB representing 14.2% of Swiss banking assets failed in March 2023; SNB injected CHF168bn liquidity (4.5% of system liabilities, below 5% threshold); taken over by UBS with a CHF9bn state guarantee. Classified non-systemic due to speedy resolution.
Federal Reserve / FDIC / US Treasury Deployed new liquidity facility, invoked systemic risk exception, and backed uninsured deposits—early intervention prevented spillover; no fiscal restructuring cost incurred.
Oil-exporting frontier economies (Angola, Chad, Azerbaijan, Equatorial Guinea, Republic of Congo) Suffered banking crises triggered by the late-2014 oil price collapse damaging bank balance sheets via deteriorating asset quality; recorded high output losses and NPLs.
Lebanon 2019 systemic crisis with deposit freezes and bank holidays; estimated overall financial-system losses ~US$70bn (over 300% of 2022 GDP), one of the most severe in the sample.
Borderline cases (Nicaragua 2018, Vietnam 2022, Sri Lanka 2023) Experienced significant stress but did not fully meet the systemic crisis definition; flagged as borderline for monitoring.
IMF / Basel Committee / BIS Provide the analytical framework and data; the database underpins cross-country vulnerability assessment, resolution-framework evaluation, and validates the effectiveness of post-GFC Basel III reforms.
What Analysts Should Monitor
  1. Monitor uninsured/wholesale deposit concentration and HTM/AFS unrealized losses at mid-sized banks because the 2023 episodes showed these drive ultra-fast, fatal deposit runs.
  2. Monitor commodity/oil price trajectories and loan-book concentration in commodity-dependent economies because terms-of-trade shocks now originate most new banking crises.
  3. Monitor the shift from central-bank liquidity support to recapitalization/restructuring because prolonged liquidity reliance often masks underlying solvency problems and inflates eventual fiscal costs.
  4. Monitor Basel III buffer headroom and whether authorities actually release CCyB/capital conservation buffers in downturns because release capacity underpins system resilience.
  5. Monitor borderline cases (Vietnam 2022, Sri Lanka 2023, Nicaragua 2018) and sovereign-bank linkages because they can escalate into full systemic crises.
What Can I Learn?
  • Liquidity support is a stress signal, not a resolution—median peak liquidity support of ~20% shows central-bank lifelines pervade crises; do not interpret them as stabilization.
  • Calibrate recovery timelines and loss assumptions by income tier: high-income crises are longer with larger GDP losses but lower NPLs/fiscal costs, while EM crises feature much higher NPLs but faster resolution.
  • Funding structure is a primary credit driver—concentrated uninsured/wholesale deposits combined with unhedged duration risk can fell a bank within days, irrespective of headline capital ratios.
  • Real-economy and commodity shocks can be leading indicators of banking distress; integrate terms-of-trade and oil-price scenarios into bank asset-quality stress tests for commodity-exposed jurisdictions.
  • Strong post-GFC capital and liquidity frameworks (Basel III buffers, LCR) plus available fiscal backstops materially reduce crisis incidence and severity—jurisdictional implementation quality should feed into sovereign and bank ratings.

Executive Summary
  • Motilal Oswal reiterates BUY on Nuvama Wealth (NUVAMA) with SoTP TP of INR1,860 (+23% upside), valuing it at 24x FY28E P/E vs current 19x FY28E P/E — flagged as the cheapest valuation in India's capital market ecosystem despite a median-range ~27% RoE.
  • Revenue/PAT projected to grow 18%/19% CAGR over FY26-28, driven by a multi-engine model: Mid-market Wealth (31% of rev), UHNI Nuvama Private (24%), Capital Markets & Asset Services (43%), and a nascent loss-making AMC/Alternates platform (2%).
  • Growth is increasingly anchored in recurring, annuity-like revenue — MPIS contributed ~61% of wealth net new money and ~59% of revenue, while Asset Services (custody/clearing) provides defensive stickiness; record FY26 net new money of INR147b.
  • Multiple recent regulatory changes — STT hike on F&O, RBI leverage tightening on securities lending, borrowing restrictions for proprietary trading, and SEBI MF TER brokerage caps (6bp cash/2bp derivatives) — are assessed as not materially impacting earnings, though second-order volume effects are possible.
  • Estimates were trimmed: PAT cut 4% for FY27/FY28 on MTM hit to wealth AUM from equity volatility and slowdown in the IE-IB (institutional equities/investment banking) business; the stock has materially underperformed (-69%/-72% relative over 6M/12M).
Key Takeaways & Analyst Lens
Key Takeaway Analyst Lens
NUVAMA is transitioning from a transaction-led model to an annuity/recurring-revenue architecture, with MPIS and ARR assets driving higher trail income and client stickiness. Higher recurring revenue mix structurally reduces earnings cyclicality and improves predictability — a credit positive that supports margin durability (CIR improving to ~55% group-wide, ~200bp wealth margin expansion expected). For rating purposes, recurring revenue depth strengthens cash flow visibility and debt-servicing capacity. Monitor the ARR vs transactional split and retention (stable ~90bp wealth, 89bp private) as the key proof points of stickiness.
Asset Services suffered a 28% YoY drop in average clearing assets after a large client exit, but rebuilt revenue to a new peak by 4QFY26 — exposing concentration risk in the custody/clearing franchise. Single-client dependence in an otherwise 'defensive backbone' segment is a latent vulnerability; the rebound is reassuring but demonstrates how lumpy institutional mandates can be. The FPI-heavy client base limits direct RBI borrowing/STT impact but creates second-order volume exposure to global flows. Monitor client concentration, clearing-asset recovery to pre-exit levels, and retention normalization (~2.5-2.6bp).
A cluster of regulatory changes (STT hike, RBI securities-lending leverage tightening, proprietary-trading borrowing restriction, SEBI TER brokerage caps) hits the capital markets ecosystem but is judged immaterial to NUVAMA earnings. Regulatory density is the dominant sector risk for brokers/wealth managers; management's benign read rests on FPI-dominated clearing clients (lower cost of funds, no Indian bank borrowing) and milder-than-proposed TER caps. The opportunity is relative resilience vs peers, but the risk is cumulative second-order volume compression. Monitor F&O volumes, cost-of-funds on the loan book, and IPO/ECM pipeline as the regulatory transmission channels.
The AMC/Alternates platform remains loss-making (operating loss widened to ~INR326m) but is positioned as medium-term optionality via private-market monetization, carry realization, and a new AMC license. Near-term earnings drag and high CIR (157% in FY26) is the cost of building scale; value hinges on fee-paying AUM growth (42% YoY to ~INR112b) and timing of private-market exits/carry — inherently lumpy and execution-dependent. The AMC license enabling SIF/retail-lite and bank distribution is the structural catalyst. Monitor loss narrowing trajectory, fund deployment/closure timelines, and NNM target delivery (INR70-80b).
Despite ~27% RoE and 18-19% earnings CAGR, the stock trades at the cheapest multiple in the peer set and has underperformed Nifty by ~72% over 12 months. The valuation-fundamentals disconnect signals either market skepticism on regulatory/cyclical risks or a genuine mispricing opportunity (the analyst's thesis). For credit work, the equity de-rating warrants checking whether it reflects deteriorating fundamentals or sentiment; net worth growth (~18% BVPS CAGR) and rising borrowings (loan book INR49b, targeting 20-30% growth) should be assessed against leverage and funding cost trends. Monitor the P/E re-rating vs peers and any equity-market MTM impact on AUM-linked fees.

What Can I Learn?
  • A high RoE paired with a depressed multiple (NUVAMA ~27% RoE at 19x P/E vs ~72% relative underperformance) is a flag to investigate whether the market is pricing genuine fundamental deterioration or sentiment — never assume mispricing without checking earnings quality.
