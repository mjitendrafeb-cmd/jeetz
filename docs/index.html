● positive
Large banks (SBI, HDFC) are pricing FCNR-B at 5.5-6%, 50-100bps below local retail term deposits — signalling they are testing demand at a cost advantage rather than competing aggressively; this limits near-term accretion to funding costs for large banks.		Track FCNR-B deposit rate vs. 3yr retail TD spread: currently -100bps for SBI, -100bps for HDFC Bank. Widening below local deposit cost improves blended cost of funds by ~5-15bps if $10bn+ is mobilised.
● negative
Smaller banks (AU SFB, Yes Bank) offering 7%+ on FCNR-B deposits to win clients signals a structural funding vulnerability — they cannot rely on brand or balance sheet scale to attract NRI flows at competitive cost, raising their marginal cost of foreign currency liabilities.		Monitor AU SFB and Yes Bank FCNR-B deposit share as % of total liabilities; 7% FCNR-B cost vs. 4.85% borrowing cost at 10x leverage = 2.15% net spread — thin buffer against FX or rate volatility. Watch NIM compression if swap costs shift.
● positive
RBI bearing the full hedging cost in 2026 (vs. a fixed 3.5% in 2013) materially improves bank economics and customer IRRs; NRI equity of $1mn at 10x leverage with large banks yields 18% US$ IRR, rising to 29% with smaller banks — structurally strong incentive to mobilise inflows.		RBI hedge subsidy eliminates FX risk for banks on eligible deposits; track RBI's contingent liability on hedging book if India-US 3yr spread moves beyond current 2.4% — every 50bps adverse move increases RBI cost by ~$340mn per $68bn mobilised.
● watch
India-US rate spread has compressed to ~2.4-2.7% across tenors (vs. ~8.5% in Aug-2013), making the 2026 scheme structurally harder to market on yield carry alone; success depends on IRR amplification through leverage (7-10x), not rate arbitrage.		India-US 3yr spread at 2.4% vs. 8.5% in 2013 reduces organic pull; monitor subscription pace vs. 2013 benchmark ($34bn = 12% of then-$275bn reserves). $68bn target = 10% of $680bn — achievability contingent on leverage uptake by NRI borrowers.
● neutral
Inclusion of rollovers of maturing deposits (unlike 2013 which was fresh-only) inflates headline mobilisation numbers — credit analysts must strip out rollovers to assess net new forex inflow actually adding to reserves.		Distinguish gross FCNR-B mobilisation from net fresh inflows; if 30-40% of reported flows are rollovers, actual reserve accretion could be $40-50bn vs. headline $68bn target — track RBI weekly forex reserve data for real-time validation.
● watch
NRE deposits currently dominate NRI deposit mix at ~60% vs. FCNR-B at ~20% — a successful FCNR-B drive risks cannibalising NRE flows (fully repatriable, INR-denominated), which would be a neutral-to-negative shift in banks' liability structure without net forex benefit.		Track quarterly NRI deposit mix (NRE vs. FCNR-B vs. NRO) in RBI data; if FCNR-B share rises above 30% at expense of NRE, net forex gain may be overstated. Banks with high NRE share (HDFC Bank, SBI) face internal cannibalisation risk.

What Can I Learn?
  • When a central bank bears the full hedging cost on a foreign currency deposit scheme, the credit benefit accrues entirely to banks and depositors, but the sovereign absorbs FX risk — analysts must model RBI's contingent liability, not just bank-level economics, to assess systemic risk.
  • Compressed India-US rate spreads (2.4% in 2026 vs. 8.5% in 2013) mean leverage, not carry, drives the product proposition; credit analysts should evaluate FCNR-B schemes through an IRR-on-equity lens rather than traditional deposit yield comparisons.
  • Scheme design differences (fresh-only vs. fresh+rollover eligibility) can materially inflate headline mobilisation metrics — always decompose reported inflows into net fresh vs. rollover to assess real impact on forex reserves and bank liability growth.
  • Smaller banks pricing FCNR-B deposits at 100bps premium to large peers reveals a structural franchise discount in cross-border deposit markets; this is a leading indicator of funding cost divergence that should be reflected in bank credit differentiation models.

What Can I Learn?
  • West Asia geopolitical stress has become a normalised risk factor in corporate India's credit narrative: companies across autos, consumer staples, building materials, and banks all referenced the Gulf war impact but universally guided for managed containment. For credit analysts, this signals that geopolitical risk discount has been priced in operationally — the next rating event will likely come from a secondary shock (crude above USD 110/bbl, INR depreciation past 90/$, or shipping lane closure) rather than first-order exposure.
  • India's power sector capex cycle has crossed a leverage inflection point: cumulative debt commitments from JSW Energy (INR 1.25trn peak), NTPC Green (INR 1.4trn FY27-FY29), Tata Power, CESC, and Torrent Power imply total sector debt additions exceeding INR 4-5trn by FY30. Credit analysts must now model transmission risk as a binary execution variable — projects commissioned without grid connectivity are stranded assets, not earnings contributors. The PGCIL pipeline (INR 1.1trn) is the rate-limiting factor for the entire sector's cash flow realisation.
  • The ECL provisioning transition under Indian Accounting Standards is a near-term capital event that will differentiate banks by portfolio quality in FY27: IndusInd's 125-150bps loan impact (highest disclosed) reflects its MFI/unsecured/vehicle concentration. Banks with cleaner corporate-heavy books (Karur Vysya, Axis) face materially lower transition charges. Rating committees should treat ECL transition as a one-time capital adequacy stress test — not a recurring credit quality signal — and adjust for it in through-the-cycle RoA assessments.
  • Commodity inflation (crude, aluminium, copper, polymers) is being systematically passed through across sectors, but with sector-specific lags that create temporary margin compression windows: building materials and adhesives (Pidilite 12% price hike, Berger 11-12%, Cera 12-16%), auto ancillaries, and consumer durables have all implemented pricing actions in Q4FY26/Q1FY27. For credit work, the key risk is not the inflation itself but the lag between cost increases and realised pricing — companies with fixed-price contracts (EPC, government supply) are most exposed and merit closer working capital and interest coverage monitoring in FY27.

Post-GFC Basel III reforms (CCyB, LCR, capital conservation buffer) demonstrably prevented a wave of banking crises through COVID-19 and the 2022 inflation surge — validating the credit-positive impact of stronger regulatory capital for bank issuers globally, but raising the bar for what constitutes a 'sufficient' capital buffer in stress analysis.		Track CCyB release decisions and LCR ratios; Basel III compliance reduced systemic crisis frequency over 2020-2025 despite three overlapping macro shocks. No new G20 banking crisis was recorded in this period.
● watch
Median gross fiscal cost of systemic banking crises is 6.7% of GDP for high-income countries and 7.5% for low/middle-income countries — sovereign credit analysts must explicitly model contingent banking-sector liability of this magnitude as a tail risk, especially where NPLs are rising or bank concentration is high.		Fiscal cost median: 6.7% GDP (high-income), 7.5% GDP (low/middle-income); peak NPL median: 11.1% (high-income) vs. 30% (low/middle-income). Budget headroom below 7% of GDP in stressed sovereigns is a red flag.
● neutral
SVB, Signature Bank, and Credit Suisse all failed in 2023 but NONE met the systemic crisis threshold — early policy intervention (FDIC systemic risk exception, SNB CHF 168bn liquidity) capped contagion. For credit analysts, speed and credibility of lender-of-last-resort response, not just bank size, is the decisive variable in preventing systemic escalation.		Peak US liquidity support was ~1% of total bank liabilities (threshold = 5%). SNB support was ~4.5% of Swiss banking liabilities — just below the 5% threshold. Monitoring central bank facility activation speed is a leading systemic indicator.
● negative
High-income country crises last longer (median close to 5 years) with larger output losses (median 31.75% of GDP) despite lower NPLs — suggesting deep financial integration amplifies credit cycle downturns even when bank balance sheets are cleaner. This directly informs through-the-cycle PD assumptions for financial sector exposures in advanced economies.		Median output loss: 31.75% of GDP (high-income) vs. 10.80% (low/middle-income). ● negative

What Can I Learn?
  • Fiscal cost of a banking crisis is not recoverable in the short run — median gross outlay is 6.7-7.5% of GDP but gross costs exclude guarantees, meaning the ex-ante sovereign contingent liability is substantially larger. A credit analyst who models only explicit recapitalisation costs is systematically underestimating sovereign risk in banking stress scenarios.
  • The 2023 US and Swiss bank failures are a masterclass in why 'systemic' is a policy outcome, not just a balance sheet condition: SVB held $367bn in deposits yet was not systemic because intervention was fast and targeted. For credit work, monitoring the credibility and speed of the lender-of-last-resort — not just bank size — is the right early-warning variable.
  • High-income countries consistently show longer crisis duration (5+ years) and larger output losses (median 31.75% of GDP) than emerging markets, despite better-capitalised banks. The mechanism is financial depth: deeper credit markets transmit bank solvency stress more broadly to the real economy. Through-the-cycle PD models for financial sector exposures in advanced economies must use longer, not shorter, stress horizons.
  • The 2014-2015 oil price shock shows that banking crises in commodity-dependent economies are triggered by sector-level revenue collapse, not financial sector excesses — yet the resolution toolkit (liquidity support, recapitalisation) is identical. Credit analysts covering resource-sector-concentrated banks should stress-test using commodity price scenarios as the primary shock, with banking system stress as the secondary, endogenous output.
