›ARC Analyst Handbook: RBI Asset Reconstruction Companies Directions, 2025

20-chapter reference handbook distilling the RBI (Asset Reconstruction Companies) Directions, 2025 (DoR.FIN.REC.No.293/26.03.001/2025-26, dated 28 Nov 2025, updated to 10 Mar 2026); ARCs are regulated…28 Nov 2025
📌 Crux of the Report
  • 20-chapter reference handbook distilling the RBI (Asset Reconstruction Companies) Directions, 2025 (DoR.FIN.REC.No.293/26.03.001/2025-26, dated 28 Nov 2025, updated to 10 Mar 2026); ARCs are regulated under SARFAESI (Sections 3, 9, 10, 12, 12A), not the RBI Act, and a single CoR covers both securitisation and asset reconstruction.
  • Capital gateway: minimum NOF of ₹300 crore (ongoing; legacy ARCs had until 31 Mar 2026), with a March 2026 amendment letting quarterly profits count toward Owned Fund via EPt = NPt − 0.25×D×t; the IBC Resolution Applicant role needs ₹1,000 crore NOF plus a 5-year control-exit sunset; deposit-taking is absolutely prohibited and bilateral acquisitions from sponsors/lenders/group entities are banned (open auctions permitted).
  • Acquisition and realisation mechanics: Board-approved acquisition policy within 90 days of CoR, at least 2 weeks pre-bid diligence, a 60%-by-value secured-creditor consent threshold for enforcement, a 6-month Planning Period (assets treated as standard), and a 5-year base / 8-year maximum realisation ceiling that carries over unreset even on ARC-to-ARC secondary sales.
  • Reconstruction toolkit: management change/takeover needs the ARC's claim ≥25% of borrower total assets plus 60% SR-holder consent, IAC review, 60-day notice and a reasoned Board order in 30 days; settlements above ₹1 crore require IAC-plus-independent-director committee governance (fraud/wilful-defaulter cases regardless of size, with a Para 62 NPV-≥-realisable-security-value floor); debt-to-equity conversion is capped at 26% of post-conversion equity unless a four-condition exemption (half-independent Board, majority-ID committee, monthly MTM, NOF compliance) is met.
  • Security Receipts: privately placed, QB-only, generally below-investment-grade hybrids issued via ARC-trusteed trusts; the ARC must retain the higher of 15% of transferors' SR investment or 2.5% of total SRs per class until full redemption; recovery ratings (a recovery-percentage band, not a default rating) are due within 6 months of acquisition, reviewed each 30 June/31 December with 6-cycle (3-year) minimum CRA retention; NAV = face value × the ARC's chosen point within the CRA band (judgement risk).
  • Prudential rules: single CRAR of NOF/RWA ≥15% with a flat 100% risk weight on virtually all core assets (0% only on cash/G-secs/other-ARC shares, 50% on contingents); the ARC's own NPA clock is 180 days (vs 90 for NBFCs) with sub-standard ≤12 months, doubtful thereafter, and automatic Loss at 36 months NPA or on breach of the 5/8-year realisation ceiling; provisioning is security-coverage-driven — 10% sub-standard, 100% on the uncovered doubtful portion plus 50% on the covered portion, 100%/write-off for Loss, and no standard-asset provision.
  • Earnings quality is deliberately suppressed: SR yield/upside is recognised only after full redemption of the entire SR principal (no accrual), and management fee — computed on NAV at the lower end of the CRA range, capped at acquisition value, payable only from recoveries — must be reversed if unrealised within 180 days or immediately if SR NAV falls below 50% of face value, with unrealised fee deducted from NOF for Ind-AS ARCs; ECL runs against an IRACP prudential floor via a non-distributable Impairment Reserve.
  • Governance and disclosure: prior RBI approval for every director/MD-CEO (90-day PRAVAAH lead time), mandatory independent Board Chair for all ARCs, MD/CEO caps of 70 years age / 5-year terms / 15-year continuous tenure with 3-year cooling-off, sponsor fit-and-proper with FATF-jurisdiction investors capped below 20% voting power; the Para 150 schedule mandates 18 balance-sheet disclosures (including auto-flags for >20% disposal discounts and >20% SR value declines), plus monthly public SARFAESI-possession disclosure and an FPC with 8am–7pm recovery calling hours and ₹5,000/day document-release delay compensation.
Key Takeaways & Analyst Lens
TakeawayAnalyst Lens
● watch
An ARC below ₹300 crore NOF faces prohibition on incremental business — existential for a pure-recovery model — and the quarterly-profit inclusion route (EPt = NPt − 0.25×D×t, March 2026 amendment) makes Owned Fund composition more volatile and audit-dependent.		NOF is the single capital gateway (no SBR-style layers) and cross-holdings in group entities or other ARCs beyond 10% of Owned Fund are netted out, so a strong headline Owned Fund can collapse into a weak NOF. Track NOF trend versus ₹300 crore and the share of NOF resting on quarterly profits rather than paid-up capital.
● negative
The 5-year base / 8-year maximum realisation ceiling is a forcing function: any asset (including via ARC-to-ARC sale, which inherits the original clock) unrealised past the ceiling is automatically classified Loss and fully provided/written off regardless of collateral quality.		A book with heavy vintages approaching year 5-8 faces a wall of compulsory write-offs even on genuinely recoverable assets; routine Board extensions to 8 years also delay QBs' Section 7(3) enforcement rights. Track the vintage distribution of acquired assets against the 5/8-year clock and the share of Loss classifications that are timeline-driven versus collateral-driven.
● neutral
ARC reported earnings structurally lag economic recovery: SR yield/upside is recognised only after full redemption of the entire SR principal, and management fee reverses if unrealised in 180 days or instantly when SR NAV drops below 50% of face value — with unrealised fee deducted from NOF for Ind-AS ARCs.		A young ARC can show thin profits while building real recovery value, and conversely a fee-heavy P&L can be hollow if collections lag. Track the Para 150(18) unrealised management-fee ageing (especially >1-year and >3-year buckets) and scheme-level collection efficiency against realisation plans, not just reported P&L.
● watch
Capital adequacy is mechanically fragile: a single 15% CRAR test on NOF with a flat 100% risk weight on essentially the whole core asset base means CRAR compresses near-1:1 with book growth and offers almost no risk-weight-mix management lever.		Growth is capital-intensive and CRAR tells you about capital, not asset quality (unlike NBFCs where RW mix encodes quality). Track CRAR headroom above 15% against planned acquisition volume, plus NOF-eroding items: provisioning hits, unrealised-fee deductions, and fair-value losses (gains are excluded, losses included — asymmetric).
● watch
SR recovery ratings are not credit ratings and NAV embeds management judgement: NAV = face value × a percentage the ARC itself selects within a ±10pp-wide CRA recovery band, reviewed only half-yearly (30 June/31 December).		For any lender holding SRs, published NAV is a judgement-laden, lagging number — two ARCs with identical CRA bands can report different NAVs. Track recovery-rating migration across half-yearly reviews and whether the ARC consistently picks the top of its band without realised-collection support; CRA changes inside the mandatory 6-cycle (3-year) retention window are a rating-shopping red flag.
● watch
Doubtful-asset provisioning swings entirely on the 'estimated realisable value of security' — 100% on the uncovered portion plus 50% on the covered portion — making collateral valuation quality a live provisioning input every period, not just an acquisition-stage control.		An ARC carrying stale or optimistic realisable-value assumptions can materially understate provisions (e.g., ₹100 cr doubtful at 60% coverage needs ₹70 cr provision; at 30% coverage, ₹85 cr). Track provision coverage ratio trend, disclosed covered/uncovered splits, and revaluation recency on large doubtful exposures.
● neutral
The 180-day regulatory NPA clock (twice the NBFC 90-day standard) coexists with Ind-AS 109's 90-day default presumption, so Ind-AS ARCs generally recognise Stage 3 impairment earlier than regulatory NPA — any later default definition needs ACB approval and disclosure, and SICR recognition cannot be deferred beyond 60 days past due.		The accounting-versus-regulatory gap is a disclosure-rich diagnostic: the Annex VI ECL-vs-IRACP comparison and the Impairment Reserve (non-distributable, non-capital) reveal whether ECL models are running lighter than the prudential floor. Track the size and trend of Impairment Reserve appropriations and disclosed >90-day-not-impaired accounts.
● watch
Related-party and captive-vehicle risk is the framework's central anti-abuse theme: bilateral acquisitions from sponsor banks, ARC lenders/fund subscribers and group entities are banned (auctions only), management fee may be paid only out of actual recoveries, and enforcement-acquired land/buildings must be disposed of within 5 years.		Historic ARC failure modes are sponsor-captive balance-sheet laundering and becoming an accidental real-estate/PE vehicle. Track Para 150 Item 1 seller concentration (dominance of a single sponsor bank even via 'auctions' warrants auction-design scrutiny), year-wise land/building holdings against the 5-year window, and debt-conversion equity stakes as a percentage of NOF.
● positive
Governance requirements exceed the NBFC middle-layer standard — universal independent Board Chair, prior RBI approval for every director appointment, 70-year age cap, 5-year terms with a 15-year continuous-tenure ceiling and 3-year cooling-off, and NRC scrutiny extending to sponsors — because ARC economics rest on hard-to-verify judgement calls (NAV selection, settlement NPV, takeover grounds).		Governance quality is the most reliable differentiator between ARCs with similar headline metrics. Track independent-chair genuineness, MD/CEO tenure against the 15-year ceiling with succession planning, IAC member independence, and whether fraud/wilful-defaulter settlements get full Tier-1 committee governance irrespective of size.
● neutral
The Para 150 schedule of 18 mandatory balance-sheet disclosures is the richest single diagnostic on any ARC, including automatic threshold-triggered flags: assets disposed at >20% discount to prior-year valuation and SRs whose value has declined >20% below acquisition value.		Read items together: acquisition (Item 5) vs realisation (Item 6) vs backlog (Item 7) trended over 3+ years reveals whether the pipeline is outrunning recovery capacity; Items 13-14 surface material valuation misses without digging; monthly public SARFAESI-possession disclosure on the ARC's website is a free primary-source cross-check on specific exposures.

What Can I Learn?
  • For ARCs, always check the realisation-timeline clock before collateral analysis: the 5/8-year ceiling silently drives mandatory Loss classification — the most severe consequence in the framework — and secondary ARC-to-ARC sales inherit, not reset, the original 8-year clock.
  • Treat SR NAV and recovery ratings as judgement-laden estimates, not observed prices: NAV is the ARC's chosen point inside a ±10pp CRA band updated only half-yearly, so build an independent recovery model for any material SR exposure and track band-selection behaviour across schemes.
  • ARC earnings analysis inverts NBFC analysis: with SR yield locked until full redemption and management fee on an aggressive 180-day/NAV-below-50% reversal regime, judge the franchise on scheme-level collection efficiency against realisation plans, not reported P&L.
  • An ARC's CRAR carries no asset-quality information — the flat 100% risk weight makes RWA nearly proportional to gross book size — so asset quality must be read separately through sub-standard-to-doubtful migration rates and security-coverage-driven provisioning adequacy.

Key Takeaways & Analyst Lens
TakeawayAnalyst Lens
● neutral
RBI's 28 Nov 2025 overhaul split the 2023 consolidated SBR Master Direction into 36 themed MDs without tightening substance — the four-layer SBR pyramid, 15% CRAR / 10% Tier-1 / 9% CET-1 (UL), the ₹1,000 cr ML threshold and 25%/40% concentration caps are all preserved, so there is no new capital burden, only a citation and compliance-mapping shift.		Rating reports issued after 28 Nov 2025 must cite the specific themed MD (the 2023 SBR MD is repealed); the brand-new instruments to know cold are Concentration Risk Mgmt, Credit Risk Mgmt, Resolution of Stressed Assets, and Climate Finance & Green Deposits. Metric to track: issuer compliance gap-analysis against the new themed MDs, especially the now-mandatory Board-approved concentration policy that extends to Base Layer entities.
● watch
The NPA glide path reaches final convergence at >90 days overdue by 31 March 2026 (from 120 days at March 2025), which mechanically inflates GNPA prints for legacy Base Layer NBFCs still on longer triggers — compounded by day-end recognition, cross-default contamination across all facilities of a borrower, and upgrade only on full clearance of arrears.		Reported GNPA jumps at smaller NBFCs in FY26 may be reclassification, not new stress — normalise before downgrading. The leading indicator is SMA-2 stock (61-90 DPD) at quarter-end, which telegraphs next quarter's NPA print; also watch restructured-standard books, which stay sub-standard until 12 months of satisfactory performance and are the classic evergreening channel.
● negative
The 125% risk weight on unsecured consumer credit and credit-card receivables (Nov 2023, preserved in the 2025 MD) is a permanent capital headwind — it cut effective CRAR by 100-200 bps overnight for consumer-heavy NBFCs — and is paired with mandatory Board-approved sub-segment exposure caps that are strictly binding, not advisory.		Consumer-finance and digital-lending NBFCs face structurally higher capital consumption per rupee of growth; the same MD treats vehicle top-up loans as unsecured and caps FLDG at 5% of portfolio (cash/HQLA only). Track equity-only Tier-1 after stripping perpetual debt (PDI can be up to 15% of Tier-1) — a 15.5% headline CRAR can conceal ~9% pure-equity Tier-1.
● watch
The Resolution of Stressed Assets MD hardwires teeth into workout timelines: 30-day Review Period from default, ICA binding at 75% by value and 60% by number, 180-day implementation deadline, and additional specific provisioning of 20% at 180 days plus 15% at 365 days (35% cumulative) for delayed plans — a direct P&L and capital penalty for slow resolution.		Any 'Additional Specific Provisioning' line in an NBFC's financials means large exposures have blown the 180/365-day deadlines — identify which. For project finance, DCCO extension headroom is 4 years (infra, court delay), 3 years (infra, other), 2 years (non-infra), 1 year (CRE); serial DCCO extensions on the same project are the strongest single early-warning signal of stress.
● negative
Microfinance is the sector's live stress point: industry GNPA at some NBFC-MFIs rose from ~2% (Mar 2024) to 6%+ (Mar 2025) on household over-leverage that breached the 50% FOIR cap via under-reported multi-lender debt, with co-lending bank partners pulling back and adding funding stress on top of asset-quality stress.		The ₹4.2 lakh cr microfinance book (₹3 lakh household-income definition, collateral-free) carries tail risks models must stress explicitly: political loan-waiver cycles (Karnataka 2024), 2-3 state geographic concentration, and monsoon/calamity shocks. Track state-wise exposure mix and FOIR policy conservatism (issuers moving to 40% Board caps are de-risking); divergence between monthly collection efficiency and DPD buckets signals slow degradation.
● neutral
Off-balance-sheet structures give far less credit relief than headlines suggest: a ₹400 cr securitisation releasing ₹360 cr of assets nets only ~₹14 cr of capital after the 10% MRR retained tranche is fully deducted, and co-lending (80:20 with banks) leaves the NBFC holding full origination, servicing, FLDG and partner-concentration risk on the entire volume.		Rate co-lending and securitising NBFCs on full origination volume and operational risk, not the retained book; a bank partner exit can choke growth overnight and FLDG is contingent capital absorption. Reconcile disclosed capital × CRAR against implied RWA including CCFs on undrawn committed lines (50% for >1-year irrevocable) — ignoring undrawn commitments can understate capital consumption by 60%.

What Can I Learn?
  • Rate the headroom, not the technical pass: an ML NBFC at 24% single-borrower exposure against a 25% Tier-1 cap, or a BL at 6.5x against the 7x leverage cap, is one bad quarter from breach — aggregate margin of safety across ratios matters more than any single compliant number.
  • Capital quality beats capital quantity: always recompute NOF (deducting group exposures above 10% of Owned Fund) and equity-only Tier-1 (stripping PDIs at the 15% cap) — a headline ₹40 cr Owned Fund can be ₹26 cr NOF, and rating on the headline overstates loss absorption materially.
  • SBR layer classification is sticky upward and aggregates across group NBFCs — two sister entities of ₹850 cr and ₹450 cr are both Middle Layer, so a fast-growing BL NBFC with thin capital should be rated for the ML compliance cliff (15% CRAR, CCO/CRO/ICAAP) it will hit in 6-12 months, not its current light-touch regime.
  • Triangulate issuer claims against regulator-facing filings: the NBS-2 auditor certificate, DNBS-02 prudential return, and CRILC SMA reporting reveal what RBI sees; inconsistency between disclosed SMA positions and CRILC data, or between quarterly press releases and audited annual tables, is itself a rating signal.

● positive
RBI bearing the full hedging cost in 2026 (vs. a fixed 3.5% in 2013) materially improves bank economics and customer IRRs; NRI equity of $1mn at 10x leverage with large banks yields 18% US$ IRR, rising to 29% with smaller banks — structurally strong incentive to mobilise inflows.		RBI hedge subsidy eliminates FX risk for banks on eligible deposits; track RBI's contingent liability on hedging book if India-US 3yr spread moves beyond current 2.4% — every 50bps adverse move increases RBI cost by ~$340mn per $68bn mobilised.
● watch
India-US rate spread has compressed to ~2.4-2.7% across tenors (vs. ~8.5% in Aug-2013), making the 2026 scheme structurally harder to market on yield carry alone; success depends on IRR amplification through leverage (7-10x), not rate arbitrage.		India-US 3yr spread at 2.4% vs. 8.5% in 2013 reduces organic pull; monitor subscription pace vs. 2013 benchmark ($34bn = 12% of then-$275bn reserves). $68bn target = 10% of $680bn — achievability contingent on leverage uptake by NRI borrowers.
● neutral
Inclusion of rollovers of maturing deposits (unlike 2013 which was fresh-only) inflates headline mobilisation numbers — credit analysts must strip out rollovers to assess net new forex inflow actually adding to reserves.		Distinguish gross FCNR-B mobilisation from net fresh inflows; if 30-40% of reported flows are rollovers, actual reserve accretion could be $40-50bn vs. headline $68bn target — track RBI weekly forex reserve data for real-time validation.
● watch
NRE deposits currently dominate NRI deposit mix at ~60% vs. FCNR-B at ~20% — a successful FCNR-B drive risks cannibalising NRE flows (fully repatriable, INR-denominated), which would be a neutral-to-negative shift in banks' liability structure without net forex benefit.		Track quarterly NRI deposit mix (NRE vs. FCNR-B vs. NRO) in RBI data; if FCNR-B share rises above 30% at expense of NRE, net forex gain may be overstated. Banks with high NRE share (HDFC Bank, SBI) face internal cannibalisation risk.

What Can I Learn?
  • When a central bank bears the full hedging cost on a foreign currency deposit scheme, the credit benefit accrues entirely to banks and depositors, but the sovereign absorbs FX risk — analysts must model RBI's contingent liability, not just bank-level economics, to assess systemic risk.
  • Compressed India-US rate spreads (2.4% in 2026 vs. 8.5% in 2013) mean leverage, not carry, drives the product proposition; credit analysts should evaluate FCNR-B schemes through an IRR-on-equity lens rather than traditional deposit yield comparisons.
  • Scheme design differences (fresh-only vs. fresh+rollover eligibility) can materially inflate headline mobilisation metrics — always decompose reported inflows into net fresh vs. rollover to assess real impact on forex reserves and bank liability growth.
  • Smaller banks pricing FCNR-B deposits at 100bps premium to large peers reveals a structural franchise discount in cross-border deposit markets; this is a leading indicator of funding cost divergence that should be reflected in bank credit differentiation models.

What Can I Learn?
  • West Asia geopolitical stress has become a normalised risk factor in corporate India's credit narrative: companies across autos, consumer staples, building materials, and banks all referenced the Gulf war impact but universally guided for managed containment. For credit analysts, this signals that geopolitical risk discount has been priced in operationally — the next rating event will likely come from a secondary shock (crude above USD 110/bbl, INR depreciation past 90/$, or shipping lane closure) rather than first-order exposure.
  • India's power sector capex cycle has crossed a leverage inflection point: cumulative debt commitments from JSW Energy (INR 1.25trn peak), NTPC Green (INR 1.4trn FY27-FY29), Tata Power, CESC, and Torrent Power imply total sector debt additions exceeding INR 4-5trn by FY30. Credit analysts must now model transmission risk as a binary execution variable — projects commissioned without grid connectivity are stranded assets, not earnings contributors. The PGCIL pipeline (INR 1.1trn) is the rate-limiting factor for the entire sector's cash flow realisation.
  • The ECL provisioning transition under Indian Accounting Standards is a near-term capital event that will differentiate banks by portfolio quality in FY27: IndusInd's 125-150bps loan impact (highest disclosed) reflects its MFI/unsecured/vehicle concentration. Banks with cleaner corporate-heavy books (Karur Vysya, Axis) face materially lower transition charges. Rating committees should treat ECL transition as a one-time capital adequacy stress test — not a recurring credit quality signal — and adjust for it in through-the-cycle RoA assessments.
  • Commodity inflation (crude, aluminium, copper, polymers) is being systematically passed through across sectors, but with sector-specific lags that create temporary margin compression windows: building materials and adhesives (Pidilite 12% price hike, Berger 11-12%, Cera 12-16%), auto ancillaries, and consumer durables have all implemented pricing actions in Q4FY26/Q1FY27. What Can I Learn?
  • Fiscal cost of a banking crisis is not recoverable in the short run — median gross outlay is 6.7-7.5% of GDP but gross costs exclude guarantees, meaning the ex-ante sovereign contingent liability is substantially larger. A credit analyst who models only explicit recapitalisation costs is systematically underestimating sovereign risk in banking stress scenarios.
  • The 2023 US and Swiss bank failures are a masterclass in why 'systemic' is a policy outcome, not just a balance sheet condition: SVB held $367bn in deposits yet was not systemic because intervention was fast and targeted. For credit work, monitoring the credibility and speed of the lender-of-last-resort — not just bank size — is the right early-warning variable.
  • High-income countries consistently show longer crisis duration (5+ years) and larger output losses (median 31.75% of GDP) than emerging markets, despite better-capitalised banks. The mechanism is financial depth: deeper credit markets transmit bank solvency stress more broadly to the real economy. Through-the-cycle PD models for financial sector exposures in advanced economies must use longer, not shorter, stress horizons.
  • The 2014-2015 oil price shock shows that banking crises in commodity-dependent economies are triggered by sector-level revenue collapse, not financial sector excesses — yet the resolution toolkit (liquidity support, recapitalisation) is identical. Credit analysts covering resource-sector-concentrated banks should stress-test using commodity price scenarios as the primary shock, with banking system stress as the secondary, endogenous output.
